Research Works

1. Storm water Infrastructure design in southern ontario in light of climate change:

Urban stormwater runoff management systems are typically designed to comply with established performance standards, relying on past climate data assumed to be consistent over time. However, with insights gleaned from climate change impact research, it becomes evident that these systems may soon be required to function effectively under altered climatic conditions compared to historical norms, reflecting the changing environment within the urban landscape. Changes in the magnitude and frequency of the observed hydroclimatic extremes are evident in many regions in Canada and around the globe. Consequently, new stormwater infrastructure design approaches are required to mitigate the effects of more intense and frequent flood events. The assessment of regional climate change impacts requires downscaling global climate models to finer spatiotemporal resolution and driving a hydrologic model to simulate land surface variables such as streamflow. This project assesses the impacts of climate change on the Batchawana watershed considering two innovative approaches: 1- it uses a statistical downscaling approach that maintains the spatiotemporal dependence between precipitation and temperature, and 2- it uses a large ensemble of simulations generated by the Raven model to quantify the uncertainties due to internal variability and modeling errors. Finally, current stormwater system designs were evaluated and new criteria have been proposed for future design and planning.

3. Identification of Construction Waste in India:

Monitoring construction waste is crucial for promoting sustainable development and reducing environmental impact. By keeping track of the types and quantities of waste generated, builders can identify opportunities for recycling and reusing materials, which conserves natural resources and reduces landfill use.
